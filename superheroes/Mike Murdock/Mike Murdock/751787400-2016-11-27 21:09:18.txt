'''Michael Dean "Mike" Murdock''' (born April 18, 1946) is an American [[Contemporary Christian]] singer-songwriter, [[televangelist]] and pastor of the Wisdom Center ministry based in [[Haltom City, Texas]]. Murdock preaches around the world and is best known for his promotion of [[prosperity theology]]. He often preaches with [[David Cerullo]] and [[Benny Hinn]] and has also preached with [[Tammy Faye Bakker]]. He hosts the ''Wisdom Keys with Mike Murdock'' television program.

==Life and work==
Murdock was born in [[Lake Charles, Louisiana]], to J.E. and Willie Murdock, and has one sister (Deborah Murdock-Johnson) who works for his ministry.<ref name="Hype"/><ref name="Money2Relatives"/> In 1964, Murdock graduated from [[LaGrange High School (Louisiana)|LaGrange High School]] in Lake Charles.<ref name="Hype"/> He attended [[Southwestern Assemblies of God University]] in [[Waxahachie, Texas]], but left after three semesters in 1966. In 1989, he received an [[honorary doctorate]] from [[International Seminary]] in Florida.<ref name="Hype">Darren Barbee, "Behind the Hype: Mike Murdock exaggerates his accomplishments and cites dubious statistics," ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram'', March 4, 2003</ref>

In 1966, Murdock married Linda Lormand (married from 1966–1979) and they adopted a boy, Jason, in 1979. On October 4, 1979 the couple divorced. Linda Lormand Murdock was given custody of Jason following the divorce. She is said to have had difficulty providing for the child despite her former husband's income.<ref name="Hype"/>

In addition to preaching, Murdock is known for his publications and songs.<ref>"Sunday," ''News and Courier'', 4 August 1990</ref> The ''[[Fort Worth Star-Telegram]]'' alleged that Murdock exaggerates his accomplishments and that he would have written an average of 300 songs per year based on his claims. The newspaper also reported that he had registered 76 songs with the [[American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers]], according to unofficial data kept on those groups' Web sites.<ref name="Hype"/>

In 1973, prior to his divorce, he established the Mike Murdock Evangelistic Association and began preaching and fundraising throughout the country.{{citation needed|date=July 2012}}

In 1989, as a pastor on [[The PTL Club]], Murdock gained national attention during the PTL scandal when he asked viewers to donate to [[Jim Bakker]] and [[Tammy Faye Bakker]] claiming they helped broken marriages and people.<ref>"Bakkers Ask for $1 Million Defense Fund," ''[[Orlando Sentinel]]'', 8 Aug 1989</ref><ref>"Jim, Tammy Bakker Return To Television," ''[[Harlan Daily Enterprise]], 3 Jan 1989</ref> The PTL later declared bankruptcy and Bakker went to prison for fraud related to his ministry.{{citation needed|date=July 2012}}

In 1999, the ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram'' reported Murdock refused interview requests and had previously declined to answer questions about money.<ref>"Ministry's practices questioned," ''[[Fort Worth Star-Telegram]]'', 10 Jan 1999</ref> 

In 2003, the ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram'' ran a series about Murdock giving association (donor-raised) money to select family members who were on his association's board.<ref name="Money2Relatives">Ministry gives money to Murdock’s relatives," ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram'', March 3, 2003 | http://www.trinityfi.org/press/murdock01.html</ref>

On March 3, 2003, the Associated Press reported Murdock asked followers for money to help the poor but spent more than 60% of donations on overhead, including his salary, and only a tiny amount (reported to be "legal minimums of 1% to 3%") on helping the needy, or any other 'public interest'.<ref name="Money2Self">"Evangelist Spends Most Charitable Donations on Himself," ''Associated Press'', March 3, 2003 | http://www.twcnews.com/archives/tx/austin/2003/03/02/evangelist-spends-most-chairitable-donations-on-himself-TX_63283.old.htm</ref>

In 2004, the ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram'' reported Murdock "has started a church and the law allows him to keep his financial records behind closed doors." In August 2004, the association purchased the International Faith Center in Haltom City, Texas, and changed its name to The Wisdom Center, which it operates as a church.<ref name="MovesBooks">"Televangelist Mike Murdock moves financial books behind closed doors," ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram,'' Dec. 15, 2004 | http://trinityfi.org/press/murdock04.html</ref>

On March 22, 2012, the ''Fort Worth Star-Telegram'' reported that Murdock's home, assessed at $1.5 million, was for sale.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://blogs.star-telegram.com/investigations/2012/03/haltom-city-televangelist-mike-murdocks-15-million-mansion-up-for-sale.html|title=Poverty-hating Haltom City televangelist Murdock's $1.5 million mansion for sale - Watchdog Bytes|website=blogs.star-telegram.com|access-date=2016-11-04}}</ref> The 9,943 sq ft 25-room home with built-in wine cooler located on 6.89 acres at 2010 Whitebridge Rd., Argyle, TX 76226 was first listed for sale on [[Realtor.com]] on November 29, 2011 for $3.2 million and then subsequently on September 4, 2013 (MLS 12020088) for $2.7 million.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.realtor.com/realestateandhomes-detail/2010-Whitebridge-Rd_Argyle_TX_76226_M82294-44596|title=2010 Whitebridge Rd, Argyle, TX 76226 - Home For Sale & Real Estate - realtor.com®|website=realtor.com®|access-date=2016-11-04}}</ref> According to public records, Murdock also was the owner of 2013 Whitebridge Rd.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.city-data.com/denton-county/W/Whitebridge-Road-1.html|title=Property valuation of Whitebridge Road, Argyle, TX: 2000 (Biily J Dalton), 2001 (Linda Szydlik), 2004, 2005, 2008 (Mary L Ribb), 2009 (Paul J Ezzo & Patricia A Ezzo), 2010 (Michael D Murdock), 2013 (tax assessments)|last=|first=|date=|website=www.city-data.com|publisher=|access-date=2016-11-04}}</ref>

Murdock has appeared on TBN, CBN, BET, INSP, WORD, [[Daystar Television Network]] and other television networks, and his weekly television program, Wisdom Keys with Mike Murdock. Murdock is a Founding Trustee on the Board of International Charismatic Ministries along with the late Oral Roberts{{citation needed|date=July 2012}}.

==Criticism==
Murdock was one of several [[televangelism|televangelists]] profiled by [[John Oliver (comedian)|John Oliver]], a [[British people|British]] [[comedian]], on a segment of television program ''[[Last Week Tonight with John Oliver]]'' that exposed "churches that exploit people’s faith for monetary gain". A clip of Murdock prompting his congregation to applaud the purchase of several private airplanes was featured, as well as his extolling viewers currently mired in credit card debt to add another $1,000 in credit charges in order to support his church and "wipe out your credit card indebtedness" by having faith in God.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.rawstory.com/2015/08/john-oliver-opens-a-can-of-whoop-ass-on-prosperity-gospel-christian-tv-preachers-and-its-beautiful/|title=John Oliver opens a can of whoop-ass on ‘prosperity gospel’ Christian TV preachers — and it’s beautiful|website=www.rawstory.com|access-date=2016-11-04}}</ref> <ref>{{Cite news|url=http://mic.com/articles/123961/watch-john-oliver-s-hellfire-sermon-on-american-televangelists|title=Watch John Oliver's Hellfire Sermon on American Televangelists|last=Mic|newspaper=Mic|access-date=2016-11-04}}</ref>
